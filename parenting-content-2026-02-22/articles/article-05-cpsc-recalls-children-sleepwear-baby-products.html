CPSC Recalls Alert: Check Your Home for These Unsafe Children's Products Right Now

By COS Parenting Staff | February 22, 2026

If you've got kids at home, you're already juggling a lot. The last thing you need is a product recall catching you off guard. But here's the thing: seven active recalls just hit the Consumer Product Safety Commission's radar, and some of them are serious. We're talking burn hazards, suffocation risks, and poisoning dangers. Take 15 minutes to check your home—it could make a real difference.

The CPSC has been busy flagging products that don't meet federal safety standards. Some of these recalls affect major retailers like Meijer. Others target lesser-known brands you might've ordered online. All of them matter. Here's what you need to know right now.

The Recalls You Need to Know About

Why These Recalls Matter (And Which Ones Are Most Urgent)

Some of these recalls are straightforward: if your kid has it, you need to deal with it. The Meijer sleepwear recall is the biggest by numbers—211,520 units of Lullaby Lane and MCS pajamas don't meet federal flammability standards. That's a serious burn risk. If your child wears these to bed, stop using them today and take them back to Meijer for a full refund. Easy fix.

Then there's the HiQiLi wintergreen essential oil. This one's scary because it's not just a product failure—it's a CPSC urgent warning. The oil lacks the child-resistant packaging that federal law (Reese's Law) requires. Wintergreen oil is highly toxic if swallowed, especially in small amounts. If you've got this product and kids under 5, secure it immediately and put Poison Control's number (1-800-222-1222) in your phone.

The Vevor baby swings are another urgent one. They violate the Safe Sleep for Babies Act because their incline is over 10 degrees. Safe sleep isn't just about comfort—it's about preventing suffocation. If you're using one of these swings as a napping spot, your baby needs a different option today. Same goes for the YCXXKJ bath seats: an unstable bath seat is a drowning hazard, period. Stop using it now.

The other recalls—robes, helmets, writing tablets—also need action, but they're typically not immediate use situations. Still, you should address them this week.

How to Check If You've Got These Products at Home

Here's where we get practical. Most of these products were bought online or at big-box retailers. Check your:

Bedroom closets and drawers for sleepwear, robes, and pajamas. The Lullaby Lane and MCS brands are sold at Meijer, so if you shop there, scan your kids' sleepwear for these labels. If you find them, gather them up and head to Meijer with your receipt (or your credit card statement if you paid there).

Nursery for baby gear. If you've got a Vevor swing, you probably know it—they're distinctive. Bath seats are easier to spot: are they YCXXKJ brand? Check the bottom or back of the product for the brand name.

Toy boxes and shelves for electronic toys. The KTEBO writing tablets are popular gifts. Look for that brand name. Kids' helmets for sports (bikes, skateboards, scooters) might include the Outdoor Master brand, especially if they're newer purchases.

Bathroom and medicine cabinet shelves for the wintergreen oil. If you use essential oils at home, this is critical. Even a small bottle is risky if a young child can access it.

Your email and online shopping history. If you've ordered from Amazon, Walmart, or other online retailers in the past year, check your purchase history for these brands. You might have forgotten about something you ordered for a gift or a second-hand item.

What's Next

Don't panic, but do act. Here's your action plan:

This week: Do a quick walk-through of your home. Check the Quick Reference table above. If you find any of these products, take the action listed in the "What to Do" column. For refunds, keep your receipt or credit card statement handy. For items like the baby swings and bath seats that need to be stopped immediately, consider what your backup plan is (where will your baby safely sleep or bathe?) and make that switch first.

Going forward: Follow the CPSC website (cpsc.gov) for monthly recall updates. You can also sign up for email alerts if you want recalls sent to your inbox. It's not doom-and-gloom—most products never get recalled—but it's smart parenting to stay in the loop.

And remember: recalls exist because the CPSC and manufacturers are paying attention to safety problems. When you return a recalled product, you're not just protecting your own kids—you're helping companies and regulators understand what went wrong. That matters.
