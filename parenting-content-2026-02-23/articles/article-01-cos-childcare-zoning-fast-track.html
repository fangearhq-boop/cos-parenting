COS Childcare Crisis: Zoning Fast-Track Sparks Neighborhood Debate

By COS Parenting Staff

February 23, 2026

Content Pillar: Local News

If you're a parent in Colorado Springs, you already know the drill. You call a dozen daycares, land on three waitlists, and still end up scrambling. Now the city is trying to do something about it by loosening zoning rules so more childcare facilities can open in residential neighborhoods. But the way it's happening has some homeowners asking: why the rush?

It's a real tension, and it matters to all of us. More childcare spots could be life-changing for thousands of COS families. But the fast-track timeline has neighborhoods feeling blindsided. Here's what's going on, what it means for your family, and where things stand right now.

The Numbers Behind the Crisis

Let's start with why the city is even considering this. The childcare shortage in El Paso County isn't a talking point anymore. It's a full-blown crisis backed by hard numbers.

There are roughly 47,000 children under the age of 5 in El Paso County. Licensed childcare spots? Just 18,400. That's barely enough to cover 39% of the kids who need care. If you've ever wondered why every good daycare has a waitlist stretching months or longer, that math is your answer.

And it's not just an inconvenience. A UCCS study found that 26% of parents who would otherwise be working are staying home specifically because of childcare challenges. That's more than one in four families forgoing a paycheck, a career, or both because they simply can't find a spot for their kids. In a city where housing costs keep climbing, that lost income hits hard.

The ripple effects reach further than you might think. Fort Carson is one of the largest military installations in the country, and the childcare shortage there is now classified as a military readiness issue. When service members can't find care for their kids, it affects deployment schedules, retention, and unit operations. Given that the military drives roughly 40% of the region's economy, that's not a problem Colorado Springs can afford to ignore.

The business community is paying attention, too. The Colorado Springs Chamber of Commerce and the Economic Development Corporation have both ranked childcare as a top-four policy issue. When the business leaders and military commanders in your city agree that something is broken, it's broken.

What the Zoning Changes Would Do

So here's the city's proposed solution: make it easier to open childcare facilities in neighborhoods where they're currently restricted or prohibited.

Right now, zoning rules create a web of barriers for anyone wanting to start a daycare. The proposed changes would cut through some of that red tape in two major ways.

First, "small" daycares serving 6 to 15 children would be allowed in R-1 single-family residential zones. These are your classic suburban neighborhoods, the ones with the cul-de-sacs and the HOA newsletters. Currently, opening even a modest daycare in these areas means navigating a tangle of zoning restrictions that stops most providers before they start.

Second, "large" daycares with no cap on the number of children would be permitted in R-2 two-family residential zones. These are areas already zoned for duplexes and higher-density living, so the city sees them as a natural fit for bigger childcare operations.

There's another piece that matters: the current requirement that childcare facilities sit on collector or arterial streets would be removed. That rule has been a major bottleneck because it essentially limits daycares to busy, high-traffic roads. Removing it would open up quieter residential streets to providers, which is where a lot of families actually live and where demand is highest.

From a parent's perspective, these changes could mean a daycare opening within walking distance of your house instead of a 20-minute drive across town. For a parent juggling drop-off, work, and pickup, that difference is enormous.

Why Some Neighborhoods Are Pushing Back

Here's where things get complicated. It's not that homeowners don't understand the childcare crisis. Most of them are parents themselves, or they have neighbors who are. The pushback isn't really about whether we need more childcare. It's about how the city is handling the process.

The City Planning Department put these zoning changes on a "fast track," and no one has publicly explained why the accelerated schedule was chosen. That lack of transparency has made people nervous. When the government moves faster than usual on something that affects your neighborhood, your property, and your daily life, you want to know the reason.

Making things worse, homeowners' associations in affected areas learned about the proposed changes just four days before the February 3 public meeting. Four days. If you've ever tried to organize a neighborhood response to anything in four days, you know that's essentially no notice at all. HOA boards that wanted to gather feedback from residents, review the proposal, and show up prepared were left scrambling.

The concerns from neighborhoods aren't frivolous. Residents are asking legitimate questions about traffic, parking, noise, and how facilities in residential areas would be monitored. A daycare for 15 kids means parents arriving and departing twice a day. On a quiet residential street, that's a noticeable change. Neighbors want to know: will there be pickup and drop-off guidelines? What about outdoor play noise during the day? Who enforces compliance if a facility causes problems?

City planners, childcare advocates, and downtown neighborhood groups are in active discussions about these questions. To the city's credit, the February 3 open house did happen, and the feedback from that meeting is being used to refine the proposal before it moves forward. But the initial trust deficit created by the fast-track approach hasn't fully healed.

What's Next

The February 3 open house was the first formal opportunity for public input, and the city says that feedback is actively shaping the revised proposal. The next step is a review by the Planning Commission, followed by a vote from City Council. No firm dates have been announced for either, but the fast-track designation suggests the city wants to move through these stages quickly.

If you're a parent who wants more childcare options in your neighborhood, now is the time to make your voice heard. Here's what you can do:

  • Contact your City Council representative and tell them that childcare availability directly affects your family. Personal stories carry weight in these decisions.
  • Attend the Planning Commission meeting when it's scheduled. Public comment periods are your chance to speak directly to the people who'll vote on this.
  • Talk to your HOA board about the proposal. Whether you support the changes, have concerns, or both, your HOA needs to hear from actual residents to represent your neighborhood accurately.
  • Follow the City Planning Department's updates on the Colorado Springs city website for meeting dates and revised proposals.

If you're a homeowner with concerns about how a nearby daycare might affect your street, those same channels work for you too. The goal isn't to pick sides between parents and neighborhoods. Most of us are both. The goal is to get this right: expand childcare access without steamrolling the communities that will host these facilities.

Colorado Springs needs thousands more childcare spots. That's not debatable. The question is how we get there in a way that works for everyone. COS Parenting will keep following this as it moves through Planning Commission and City Council. We'll let you know when meeting dates are announced so you can show up and have your say.
